# The Thiel Axis — Market Structure & Value Capture

The second lens. Demand tells you whether the company can grow; structure tells you
whether it can ever be *worth* anything. A growing business in a perfectly competitive
market captures none of the value it creates.

## Core doctrine

- **Competition is for losers.** Aim for monopoly; avoid hyper-competition. Capitalism
  and competition are antonyms — under perfect competition profits get bid to zero. The
  goal is to be the only one doing what you do.
- **Create value AND capture it.** A valuable company creates X dollars of value and
  captures Y% of X. The two are independent. A huge market with low capture (airlines)
  is a worse business than a smaller market with high capture (Google search). Founders
  obsess over X and ignore Y.
- **Two kinds of business: monopoly and perfectly competitive.** There is shockingly
  little in between. And founders lie in opposite directions: monopolists pretend to be
  competitive (to dodge scrutiny), competitive firms pretend to be unique (to attract
  capital). Name what you actually see.
- **Start with a small, dominable market.** Then expand concentrically. PayPal began
  with ~20k eBay power-sellers; Facebook with one campus; Amazon with books. Chasing a
  giant market on day one is evidence the founder hasn't defined the category correctly.
- **Last mover beats first mover.** Most of a company's value is in cash flows many
  years out, so durability dominates current growth rate. Be the last company in a
  category, not the first.

## The four moats — score the company on all four

1. **Proprietary technology** — meaningfully better than the next best thing (rule of
   thumb: 10x). Note: tech advantages can be superseded, so ask whether the lead lasts.
2. **Network effects** — the product gets more valuable as more people use it. These
   strengthen with time and are the most durable moat.
3. **Economies of scale** — unit costs fall as volume rises in a way competitors can't
   match.
4. **Brand** — a position in the customer's mind that can't be copied by feature parity.

## The lies founders tell about their market

- **The intersection lie** — "we're the only [X]-for-[Y] in [Z]." A market defined by
  enough qualifiers usually means *no real market* — they've narrowed until they're
  alone in an empty room.
- **The union lie** — "we compete in the $1T [tech/AI/health] market." This usually
  hides a real monopoly that's afraid to name how small and specific it actually is, or
  a competitive firm pretending the giant TAM is addressable.
- **Mimetic competition is a trap.** A crowded market is often evidence of shared
  delusion, not validation. People imitate each other into a fight over imaginary
  differences. "All happy companies are different; all unhappy companies are alike" —
  alike in failing to escape competition.

## The AI-cloneability test (2026)

**Code is no longer a moat.** A competent solo developer can clone a typical
mid-feature SaaS in a weekend with an AI coding assistant. So when scoring the four
moats, ask: *"If a competitor could rebuild this product in two weeks with Claude, what
survives?"* If the answer is "nothing," the company has no moat regardless of how clean
the codebase is.

Surviving moats now skew toward: **network effects, proprietary DATA flywheels** (not
proprietary code), **distribution / brand, deep workflow integration** with months of
switching cost, **regulatory / compliance, physical operations,** and **trust** in
high-stakes domains. "We'll execute faster" and "our code is better" are not moats.

## How to grade with this lens

Green: a credible path to monopoly in a definable small market, at least one durable
moat that survives the cloneability test. Yellow: real demand but a structurally
competitive market, or moats that are promised but not yet real. Red: a commodity in a
crowded market with no possible moat, or an idea whose entire value is software a
competitor rebuilds in a weekend.
