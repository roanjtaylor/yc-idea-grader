# The PG Axis — Product & Demand

The first lens. Read the founder's submission for one thing above all: **is there
real demand, and is the company built to grow into it?** Most early ideas die here.

## Core doctrine

- **A startup is a company designed to grow fast.** If it isn't growing, it isn't
  a startup yet — it's a project. Everything else (funding, press, hiring) is a
  consequence of growth, not a substitute for it.
- **Make something people want.** This is the whole game. There is no clever
  positioning, no growth hack, no pitch that rescues a product nobody wants. If no
  one wants it, you don't have a startup.
- **Default alive or default dead.** At any moment one of these is true. On current
  revenue growth and burn, does the company reach profitability on the money it has?
  Founders who can't answer are usually default dead and hiding from it.
- **Do things that don't scale.** Recruit users by hand, deliver the service
  manually, delight a tiny number of people. The unscalable phase ends when usage
  forces it to — not before. Founders who skip it never actually find demand; they
  build for an imagined user and launch to silence.
- **Talk to users.** The number of real user conversations is the leading indicator
  of everything else. A founder who hasn't talked to users is guessing, and the
  guesses are usually flattering.
- **Narrow wedge beats broad platform.** Always. Platforms are what wedges become
  after they survive long enough to expand. "We're building a platform for X" on day
  one is almost always a sign the founder hasn't found the one acute use case.

## Demand reality — separating real from polite

- **Surface "yes" is cheap.** People say they'd use things to be nice. Real demand
  is people who would be upset — who would scramble — if the product vanished
  tomorrow. Ask: what are they doing *today* to solve this, and what does it cost them?
- **Vanity metrics are evidence the founder is hiding from the truth.** Signups,
  impressions, waitlist counts, LOIs. The honest metrics are retention, repeat usage,
  and revenue. If a founder leads with vanity metrics, the real ones are probably bad.
- **The hair-on-fire test.** The best early customers have the problem so badly they'd
  use a clumsy v1 held together with tape. If you need a polished product to get anyone
  to care, the problem isn't acute enough yet.

## Specific failure modes to name

- **Free-incumbent switching cost.** The highest switching cost there is comes from a
  *free* incumbent the user already tolerates — email, spreadsheets, WhatsApp, paper.
  "Better than free" is not enough; people don't switch off good-enough free tools for
  marginally better paid ones. You need 10x or a workflow they can't get elsewhere.
- **Two-sided marketplaces** die from **cold-start** and **supply leakage**, in that
  order. Supply and demand have to grow together, and once both sides can find each
  other they have every incentive to transact off-platform. Name how the marketplace
  defends against both.
- **Warm-network bias.** Early traction from friends, the founder's old employer, or a
  personal network is not demand — it's goodwill. It tells you almost nothing about
  whether strangers will pay.
- **Premature scale.** Spending on growth, hiring, or paid acquisition before
  retention proves people want the thing. This converts a small problem into an
  expensive one.

## How to grade with this lens

Green: clear acute demand, evidence beyond polite interest, a genuine wedge, growth or
the credible mechanics of it. Yellow: plausible demand but unproven — the founder is
still guessing, or traction is warm-network only. Red: no real demand, a free incumbent
that won't be dislodged, or a "platform" with no acute first use case.
