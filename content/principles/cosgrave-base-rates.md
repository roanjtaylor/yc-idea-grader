# Base Rates & the Power Law of Outcomes

The third lens, and the one founders most want to skip. Demand and structure tell you
about *this* company. Base rates tell you what usually happens to companies that look
like it. The honest prior is failure.

## Core doctrine

- **The default outcome is failure or stagnation, not success.** Among YC's mature
  batches roughly a quarter are inactive (dead) and roughly a quarter are acquired or
  public; the rest grind on with unproven futures. For startups *without* YC's selection
  and support, the failure base rate is worse. Treat success as the rare event it is.
- **Outcomes are heavy-tailed.** A handful of winners produce the vast majority of all
  returns; the rest cluster near zero. Most "successful" venture-backed companies never
  become profitable — they raise, grow, and either exit or die before unit economics
  matter. Many are zombies.
- **VC capital extends life without changing fate.** A big round is not validation; it
  is a longer runway over the same cliff. Burn-funded growth with no path to profit is a
  delayed funeral, not a business.
- **Founder skill is necessary but not sufficient.** Right time × right place × right
  team × right idea — three of four is usually fatal. The same founder with the same
  idea in a different year often fails. Timing is not something you can will into
  existence.
- **Survivorship bias makes the past look deterministic.** For every Stripe there are
  hundreds of unremembered payment startups that died. Reading only winner case studies
  teaches almost nothing, because you can't see the variance — you can't see the
  identical-looking companies that lost.

## The honest prior

This startup will most likely fail. That is not pessimism; it is the base rate. The
burden of proof is on the founder to show why *this one* beats it — with evidence, not
narrative. Absent strong evidence, default to failure in your reasoning, and ask what
specific evidence would move the prior.

This is **not** a reason to refuse every idea. It is the calibration that makes a `go`
mean something. The job is to find the cheap experiment that would generate the
evidence — not to demand certainty that doesn't exist at this stage.

## Anti-mythology checks

- **The hero-founder narrative obscures the carnage.** A compelling founder story is
  not evidence. Charisma is uncorrelated with whether the market wants the product.
- **Acceptance and funding are weaker signals than they used to be.** As batch sizes and
  total venture dollars have grown, getting in or raising a round says less about the
  odds than it once did.
- **"Unprofitable by design" is a choice with a deadline.** Some companies plan to be
  unprofitable for years. That can be right — but only if there's a concrete mechanism
  by which scale flips to profit. Without it, it's the delayed-funeral pattern.

## How to grade with this lens

Green: there's specific evidence this company beats the base rate (real retention, real
willingness to pay, a structural reason it's in the tail). Yellow: nothing yet
distinguishes it from the median company that quietly dies — but a cheap experiment
could. Red: the company shows the classic doomed patterns (burn-funded growth with no
profit path, success that depends on timing the founder can't control, traction that's
pure survivorship/warm-network) with no evidence against them.
